# Arpeggio Theory Reference

Synthesized from established guitar pedagogy. Used to inform Virtuoso arpeggio generators, sweep patterns, and pathway design.

---

## Arpeggio forms and inversions

### What an arpeggio is

An arpeggio is a chord's notes played one at a time in series, like a scale. Unlike a chord (all notes simultaneously), an arpeggio can include multiple notes on the same string (whereas chords can't). Arpeggios are useful for:
- Mapping all chord-tone locations in a given position
- Building chord voicings and inversions by selecting subsets
- Composing melodic lines that outline the harmony

### Three main groups

**Major group** — bright/happy quality:

| Name | Formula | Essential tones |
|------|---------|----------------|
| Major triad | 1, 3, 5 | 1, 3 |
| Major 6th | 1, 3, 5, 6 | 1, 3, 6 |
| Major 6/9 | 1, 3, 5, 6, 9 | 1, 3, 6, 9 |
| Major 7th | 1, 3, 5, 7 | 1, 3, 7 |
| Major add9 | 1, 3, 5, 9 | 1, 3, 9 |
| Major 9th | 1, 3, 5, 7, 9 | 3, 7, 9 |
| Major 13th | 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13 | 3, 7, 13 |

Major arpeggios fit over Ionian (1), Lydian (4), and Mixolydian (5) modes.

**Minor group** — dark/sad quality:

| Name | Formula | Essential tones |
|------|---------|----------------|
| Diminished triad | 1, b3, b5 | all three |
| Minor triad | 1, b3, 5 | 1, b3 |
| Minor 6th | 1, b3, 5, 6 | 1, b3, 6 |
| Minor 6/9 | 1, b3, 5, 6, 9 | b3, 6, 9 |
| Minor 7th | 1, b3, 5, b7 | 1, b3, b7 |
| Minor 7 b5 (half-dim) | 1, b3, b5, b7 | b3, b5, b7 |
| Minor add9 | 1, b3, 5, 9 | 1, b3, 9 |
| Minor 9th | 1, b3, 5, b7, 9 | b3, b7, 9 |
| Minor 11th | 1, b3, 5, b7, 9, 11 | b3, b7, 11 |
| Minor 13th | 1, b3, 5, b7, 9, 11, 13 | b3, b7, 13 |

Minor arpeggios fit Dorian (2), Phrygian (3), Aeolian (6). Minor 7b5 fits Locrian (7).

**Dominant group** — tension-building quality:

| Name | Formula | Essential tones |
|------|---------|----------------|
| Dominant 7 | 1, 3, 5, b7 | 1, 3, b7 |
| Dom 7 sus4 | 1, 4, 5, b7 | 4, 5, b7 |
| Dominant 9 | 1, 3, 5, b7, 9 | 3, b7, 9 |
| Dominant 11 | 1, 3, 5, b7, 9, 11 | 3, b7, 11 |
| Dominant 13 | 1, 3, 5, b7, 9, 11, 13 | 3, b7, 13 |
| Sus2 | 1, 2, 5 | all |
| Sus4 | 1, 4, 5 | all |
| Sus9 | 1, 4, 5, b7, 9 | 4, b7, 9 |

Dominant arpeggios fit Mixolydian (5).

**Altered/altered dominant group:**

| Name | Formula | Essential tones |
|------|---------|----------------|
| Augmented | 1, 3, #5 | all |
| Augmented 7 | 1, 3, #5, b7 | 3, #5, b7 |
| Diminished 7 | 1, b3, b5, bb7 | all |
| Dom 7 b5 | 1, 3, b5, b7 | 3, b5, b7 |
| Major 7 b5 | 1, 3, b5, 7 | 3, b5, 7 |
| Minor/major 7 | 1, b3, 5, 7 | 1, b3, 7 |

An altered chord contains a note outside any of the seven diatonic modes — it creates more tension than an unaltered dominant.

### Essential tones principle

When it's impossible or undesirable to play all notes of an arpeggio formula, the "essential tones" (shown above) are the minimum required to preserve the arpeggio's identity. The root establishes the name; the 3rd/b3rd establishes major vs. minor quality; the 7th/b7 establishes chord function.

### Five arpeggio positions per chord type

The Guitar Method: All Scales And Arpeggios presents five example arpeggio positions for each chord type, one for each string where the root can sit (6th, 5th, 4th, 3rd, 2nd strings). These are ordered root-position ascending/descending up the neck. The pattern for a given root-string position is movable — place the root on the named note and the full arpeggio pattern follows.

In the CAGED system, each CAGED form has a corresponding arpeggio pattern that maps the chord tones across the fretboard in that shape's home zone.

### Inversions

Inversions rearrange the order of notes in an arpeggio/chord — same building blocks, different lowest note:
- Root position: 1 in bass
- First inversion: 3 in bass
- Second inversion: 5 in bass
- Third inversion (7th chords): 7 in bass

On guitar, inversions are often created by partial forms — removing fingers from a full CAGED-based chord shape leaves a partial shape with a different bass note. Each CAGED form naturally yields several useful inversions this way.

## Sweep mechanics and technique

### The roll technique

When two consecutive notes are on adjacent strings and use the same finger, **roll the fingertip** from one string to the next. Do NOT lift the finger and re-place it. This is essential for fluid arpeggio playing across adjacent strings.

### Connection across strings

On the guitar, adjacent string pairs have a 4-semitone interval (except G–B which is 3). Arpeggio patterns take advantage of this by locating chord tones efficiently per string — typically 1–2 notes per string in a sweep-style pattern.

### Upstroke and downstroke balance

When playing arpeggios with a pick, make the upstroke sound as much like the downstroke as possible. On low strings with a downstroke, there's a natural accent; on the upstroke return, favor the lower strings by bringing the pick back across them lightly. This is the sweep-picking mechanical principle.

### Augmented triad arpeggios

Augmented triads (1, 3, #5) are symmetrical in major thirds — the same arpeggio pattern repeats every 4 frets. Practice augmented triad arpeggios from all possible root positions.

### Diminished arpeggios

Fully diminished 7th (1, b3, b5, bb7) is symmetric in minor thirds — the same 4-note arpeggio repeats every 3 frets. Only 3 unique diminished 7th arpeggios exist (all other "different" ones are inversions of the same set). This means any fully diminished arpeggio can be played starting from 4 different roots with the same shape shifted by 3 frets.

## Diatonic arpeggio sequencing

### Diatonic arpeggios over a key

In any major key, the seven degrees each produce a chord and corresponding arpeggio quality:

| Degree | Quality | Arpeggio |
|--------|---------|----------|
| I | Major | Maj 7 |
| II | Minor | Min 7 |
| III | Minor | Min 7 |
| IV | Major | Maj 7 |
| V | Dominant | Dom 7 |
| VI | Minor | Min 7 |
| VII | Dim/half-dim | Min 7 b5 |

Cycling through these seven arpeggios in sequence (I through VII) in a single position is a foundational exercise — covering all diatonic keys in a given position.

### II–V–I arpeggio movement (Voice Leading)

The most important three-chord sequence in jazz: II–7 → V7 → IMaj7. Chord tone resolution rules:
- 7th of II–7 resolves to 3rd of V7
- 3rd of II–7 resolves to 7th of V7
- 7th of V7 resolves to 3rd of I
- 3rd of V7 resolves to 7th of I (or 7th can go up to root)

These guide-tone movements create smooth voice leading and can be the basis for both arpeggio exercises and improvised melodic lines.

### Chord tones as phrase anchors

Chord tones (1, 3, 5, 7) are the most natural notes to begin and end melodic phrases with. The ear hears these first and registers them as harmonically stable. Scale tones and tensions work as passing/approach notes between chord tones.

### Approach note strategies (Guitar Method: All Scales And Arpeggios — "Approaches")

When playing arpeggios in a sequence context, approaches prevent mechanical-sounding patterns:

| Approach | Pattern | Description |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| 1-1-1 | Repeat each degree 3× | Rhythmic emphasis on each chord tone |
| 1-2-3 | Ascending group of 3 | Stepwise motion into each chord tone |
| 3-2-1 | Descending group of 3 | Approach from above |
| 4-note | 4-3-2-1 or variants | Works with common 4-beat meter |
| Mixed | Combination | E.g., 1-2-3 then 1-1-1 alternating |

These same approaches apply when playing arpeggios as melodic lines. Multi-dimensional (approaches within approaches) gives advanced players endless variation.

## Practice implications for Virtuoso

1. **Start with major and minor triads.** The 1-3-5 and 1-b3-5 patterns are the foundation — all extensions add notes above this base. Virtuoso's arpeggio generator should default to triads and offer 7th/9th/etc. as extensions.

2. **Five-position coverage.** For each arpeggio type, the five root-string positions (roots on strings 6, 5, 4, 3, 2) give full fretboard coverage. Virtuoso can generate "one position per run" or "cycle all five positions" as variation options.

3. **Essential tones inform audio synthesis.** When routes.py synthesizes audio for an arpeggio exercise, prioritize root, 3rd, and 7th for chord identity. The 5th can be omitted without loss of function.

4. **II–V–I arpeggio drills are a natural exercise type.** Cycling through min7 → dom7 → maj7 arpeggios in a II–V–I pattern over a key center is a high-value jazz practice format that could be a Virtuoso pathway or exercise variant.

5. **Diatonic 7th arpeggios as a sequence exercise.** Generate all seven diatonic arpeggios in one key, one per measure, cycling through I–VII in a single CAGED position.

6. **Approaches as sequence patterns.** The 1-2-3 and 3-2-1 approaches map directly to Virtuoso's SEQUENCE_PATTERNS concept. Adding arpeggio-specific approaches (not just scale runs) would enrich the generator.

7. **Diminished and augmented symmetry = easy transposition.** Because dim7 repeats every 3 frets and augmented repeats every 4, a single arpeggio pattern covers 3 or 4 "different" arpeggios. Virtuoso can generate diminished/augmented patterns knowing the same shape works at multiple roots.
